## 🦠 Malware Overview
Malware is software designed to harm devices or networks, primarily to obtain financial gain or an intelligence advantage.

### Common Types:
* **Viruses:** Malicious code written to interfere with computer operations. Requires user initiation (e.g., opening an attachment) to execute, hide in files, and destroy data.
* **Worms:** Self-replicating malware that spreads across systems on its own without user interaction.
* **Ransomware:** A malicious attack where threat actors encrypt an organization's data and demand payment to restore access.
* **Spyware:** Malware used to secretly gather and sell information (private emails, texts, locations) without consent.

### Historical Malware Events:
1. **1986:** Alvi brothers created the Brain virus.
2. **1988:** Robert Morris created the Morris worm.
3. **2000:** Onel de Guzman created the ILOVEYOU (Loveletter) virus.
4. **2016:** A massive, successful breach of the company Equifax occurred.

## 🎭 Social Engineering
A manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables by creating an environment of false trust.

### Common Phishing & Social Engineering Attacks:
* **Phishing:** Using digital communications to trick people into revealing sensitive data.
* **Business Email Compromise (BEC):** An email impersonating a known source to make a seemingly legitimate request for financial advantage.
* **Spear Phishing:** A targeted malicious email attack against a specific user or group.
* **Whaling:** Spear phishing specifically targeting company executives.
* **Vishing:** Exploiting voice communication (phone calls) to obtain sensitive information.
* **Smishing:** Using text messages (SMS) to trick users.
* **Social Media Phishing:** Attacks initiated after collecting detailed information about a target from social platforms.
* **Watering Hole Attack:** Infecting a website frequently visited by a specific target group.
* **USB Baiting:** Strategically leaving a malware-infected USB stick for an employee to find and plug in.
* **Physical Social Engineering:** Impersonating an employee, customer, or vendor to bypass physical security.

### Core Principles of Social Engineering:
Why these attacks work on human psychology:
* **Authority:** People are conditioned to respect and follow authority figures.
* **Intimidation:** Using bullying or persuasive tactics to force compliance.
* **Consensus/Social Proof:** Tricking victims by claiming others have already complied.
* **Scarcity:** Implying goods or services are in limited supply to force action.
* **Familiarity:** Establishing a fake emotional connection.
* **Trust:** Building a relationship over time to eventually exploit it.
* **Urgency:** Persuading the victim to respond quickly without thinking.

## 🏛️ CISSP 8 Security Domains
The core framework used to organize the work of security professionals.

1. **Security and Risk Management:** Defines security goals, risk mitigation, compliance, business continuity, and laws.
2. **Asset Security:** Secures digital/physical assets, including storage, retention, and destruction of data.
3. **Security Architecture and Engineering:** Optimizes data security by ensuring effective tools, systems, and processes are in place.
4. **Communication and Network Security:** Manages and secures physical networks and wireless communications.
5. **Identity and Access Management (IAM):** Ensures users follow policies to control access to physical assets (offices) and logical assets (networks/apps).
6. **Security Assessment and Testing:** Conducting control testing, data analysis, and security audits to monitor for vulnerabilities.
7. **Security Operations:** Conducting investigations and implementing preventative measures.
8. **Software Development Security:** Enforcing secure coding practices and guidelines to create secure applications.

## ⚔️ Attack Types & Domain Mapping
* **Password Attacks (Communication & Network Security):** Attempts to access secured systems (e.g., Brute force, Rainbow table).
* **Social Engineering Attacks (Security & Risk Management):** Exploiting human error (Phishing, BEC, Baiting).
* **Physical Attacks (Asset Security):** Incidents affecting physical environments (Malicious USB cables, Card cloning/skimming).
* **Adversarial Artificial Intelligence (Comm/Network & IAM):** Manipulating AI and machine learning to conduct highly efficient attacks.
* **Supply-Chain Attacks (Multiple Domains):** Targeting a vulnerability at any point in the third-party supply chain to deploy malware.
* **Cryptographic Attacks (Communication & Network Security):** Affecting secure communications (e.g., Birthday, Collision, Downgrade attacks).

## 🕵️ Threat Actors & Hackers

### Threat Actor Types:
* **Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs):** Highly skilled actors (often nation-states) that remain undetected for long periods to damage infrastructure or steal intellectual property.
* **Insider Threats:** Authorized users who abuse their access for sabotage, corruption, espionage, or data leaks.
* **Hacktivists:** Threat actors driven by a political agenda, aiming for social change, propaganda, or demonstrations.

### Hacker Categories:
* **Authorized (Ethical) Hackers:** Follow a code of ethics and the law to conduct risk evaluations and safeguard organizations.
* **Semi-Authorized Hackers:** Researchers who search for vulnerabilities but do not exploit them maliciously.
* **Unauthorized (Unethical) Hackers:** Malicious actors who break the law to collect and sell data for financial gain.
