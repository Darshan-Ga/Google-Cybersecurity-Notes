# Module 3: Security Frameworks, Compliance, and Ethics

## 1. Security Frameworks & Controls
**Security Framework:** Guidelines used for building plans to help mitigate risks and threats to data and privacy.

**Purpose of a Security Framework:**
1. Protect PII (Personally Identifiable Information).
2. Secure financial information.
3. Identify security weaknesses.
4. Manage organizational risk.
5. Align security with business goals.

**Four Main Components of a Security Framework:**
1. Identifying and documenting security goals.
2. Setting guidelines to achieve security goals.
3. Implementing strong security processes.
4. Monitoring and communicating results.

**Security Controls:** Safeguards designed to reduce specific security risks. They are used alongside frameworks to ensure processes are implemented correctly and compliance is met.

**Asset:** An item perceived as having value to an organization.

---

## 2. The CIA Triad
A foundational model that helps inform how organizations consider risk when setting up systems and security policies.
* **Confidentiality:** Only authorized users can access specific assets or data.
* **Integrity:** Data is correct, authentic, and reliable.
* **Availability:** Data is accessible to those who are authorized to access it.

## 3. Compliance Standards & Frameworks
**Compliance** is the process of adhering to internal standards and external regulations.

* **NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology):** A U.S.-based agency that develops voluntary compliance frameworks to manage risk. Includes the **NIST CSF** (Cybersecurity Framework) and **NIST RMF** (Risk Management Framework).
* **FERC-NERC:** Regulation for organizations working with the U.S./North American power grid. Legally requires adherence to Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards.
* **FedRAMP:** U.S. federal program standardizing security assessment and monitoring of cloud services.
* **CIS (Center for Internet Security):** A nonprofit providing actionable controls to safeguard systems against attacks and establish defense plans.
* **GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation):** E.U. regulation protecting E.U. residents' data privacy. Requires organizations to report breaches to affected E.U. citizens within **72 hours**.
* **PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard):** International standard to secure credit card information processing and reduce fraud.
* **HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act):** U.S. federal law (1996) protecting patients' Protected Health Information (PHI). Governed by Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification rules. *(Note: HITRUST is a framework that helps institutions meet HIPAA compliance).*
* **ISO (International Organization for Standardization):** Establishes international standards across borders for technology, manufacturing, and management.
* **SOC 1 & SOC 2 (System and Organizations Controls):** Developed by AICPA. Reports focusing on user access policies, financial compliance, risk, and overall data safety to prevent fraud.

## 4. Security Ethics & Privacy
**Security Ethics:** Guidelines for making appropriate decisions as a security professional. You must remain unbiased, transparent, rely on evidence, and respect the law.

**Key Concepts:**
* **Confidentiality:** Maintaining a high level of respect for privacy to safeguard assets.
* **Privacy Protection:** Safeguarding personal information from unauthorized use.
  * **PII (Personally Identifiable Information):** Info used to infer identity (e.g., name, phone number).
  * **SPII (Sensitive PII):** Stricter handling guidelines (e.g., Social Security Numbers, credit card numbers).
* **Laws:** Rules recognized and enforced by a governing entity.

---

## 5. The Ethics of Counterattacks
Counterattacks by private organizations or individuals are strongly discouraged and generally illegal. 

**United States Standpoint:**
* Deploying a counterattack is **illegal** (e.g., Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986).
* You can only defend. Counterattacking is considered vigilantism.
* Threat actors often use innocent systems; counterattacking can cause collateral damage or trigger international incidents (if state-sponsored).
* Only approved federal government or military personnel can legally counterattack.

**International Standpoint (ICJ Guidelines):**
Counterattacks are theoretically permitted *only* if:
1. It only affects the party that attacked first.
2. It is a direct communication asking the initial attacker to stop.
3. It does not escalate the situation.
4. The effects can be reversed.
*(Note: Because these parameters are almost impossible to guarantee in cyber warfare, organizations typically do not counterattack).*
